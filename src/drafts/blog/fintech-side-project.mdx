There's a romantic version of the startup origin story where the founder quits their job, maxes out their credit cards, and builds something world-changing in a garage. The reality for most of us — especially those with mortgages of our own and three kids — is messier.

Fundhaus started as a question I couldn't stop thinking about: why is mortgage refinancing so unnecessarily painful when the data to automate most of it already exists? That question turned into research, then a business plan, then code — all while I continued running technology at one of Australia's largest mortgage aggregators.

## The discipline of narrow scope

The hardest lesson was ruthless scoping. When you have domain expertise, you see opportunities everywhere. "We should also handle purchases." "What about investment loans?" "We could add a comparison tool."

Every one of those ideas is valid. And every one of them would have killed the project.

Fundhaus does one thing: refinancing for PAYG employees with LVR under 80%. That's it. A narrow wedge into a massive market. The constraint isn't a limitation — it's what makes the product buildable as a side project and defensible as a business.

## Time management is product management

With limited hours, every architectural decision becomes a time management decision. I chose Tailwind and shadcn/ui not because they're trendy, but because they let me build a polished UI without making design decisions at 11pm after the kids are asleep.

I chose a monorepo not because it's elegant, but because context-switching between repositories burns cognitive cycles I don't have to spare.

The technology choices that matter most for a side project aren't the ones that scale best — they're the ones that preserve your energy for the decisions that actually matter.

## When does it stop being a side project?

That's the question I'm wrestling with now. There's a natural tension between the safety of a salary and the pull of something you believe in deeply enough to write a business plan and chase funding for.

I don't have the answer yet. But I'm learning that the question itself is a signal worth paying attention to.
