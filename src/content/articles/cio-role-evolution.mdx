Five years ago, a CIO's primary job was keeping the lights on. Uptime, security, infrastructure, vendor management. Important work, but fundamentally operational.

That version of the role is disappearing — not because those responsibilities went away, but because they've become table stakes. The CIOs who are thriving now are the ones who've shifted from "how do we run technology" to "how does technology run the business."

## From cost centre to competitive advantage

The shift is most visible in how CIOs relate to revenue. The old model was cost containment: keep IT spend as a percentage of revenue as low as possible. The new model is investment: where can technology create margin, speed, or capabilities that competitors can't match?

In mortgage aggregation, this means the difference between maintaining a broker portal and building intelligent matching algorithms that measurably improve settlement rates. Same budget, completely different strategic value.

## The skills that matter now

Technical depth still matters — you can't lead engineers effectively without understanding their craft. But the skills that differentiate great CIOs in 2025 are increasingly non-technical: storytelling to a board, financial modelling for business cases, talent strategy in a competitive market, and the ability to translate between business problems and technical solutions without losing fidelity in either direction.

The CIO who can explain to a CEO why a platform migration will unlock three new revenue streams — in language the CEO actually cares about — will always outperform the one who talks about microservices and Kubernetes.

## What's next

I think the CIO role is converging with the CPO (Chief Product Officer) role in many organisations, especially in financial services where the product *is* the technology. The leaders who recognise this convergence early and build skills across both domains will define the next generation of executive leadership in tech-enabled businesses.
