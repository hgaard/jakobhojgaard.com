**The throughline:** You're a founder building a product in finance. You don't have a team of 20. Instead, you have Claude, Claude Code, and a growing set of AI agents handling development, research, analysis, and admin. Each post is an honest check-in on how that's actually going.

## Recurring Post Format

Each post follows the same loose structure to give the series consistency and make it easy for readers to follow across instalments.

### 1. The Setup

What problem or task were you facing? Why did you turn to AI for this instead of hiring, outsourcing, or doing it manually? This grounds each post in a real business need.

*Key consideration: keep this relatable. Readers should recognise the problem before you introduce the AI solution.*

### 2. The Build

How you actually set things up. Enough detail that someone could learn from your approach, but told as a narrative rather than a step-by-step tutorial. Include screenshots, prompt snippets, or workflow diagrams where they help — but the focus is on the decisions and tradeoffs you made.

*Key consideration: balance specificity with readability. Too vague and it's hand-wavy. Too detailed and it reads like documentation.*

### 3. The Reality Check

What actually happened. Where did it exceed expectations? Where did it fall short? Where did you have to step in? This is the section that earns trust and separates the series from "AI is magic" content.

*Key consideration: be genuinely honest. The failures and workarounds are more interesting than the wins.*

### 4. The Takeaway

One or two reflections. Not cheerleading, not doom. The nuanced stuff — like "this saved me 10 hours a week but I spent 3 hours building the right prompt structure first."

*Key consideration: end with something actionable or thought-provoking, not just a summary.*

> **YOUR TURN:** Does this four-part structure feel right? Would you add anything — maybe a "cost/time" breakdown, or a "what I'd do differently" section? What tone are you going for — conversational, technical, reflective?

How you've set up Claude and Claude Code as your core product development workflow. This is the natural opener because it's the most fundamental decision — you're building the product itself with AI.

**What to cover:**

- You're not naming the product yet — keep descriptions of what you're building general (e.g. "a fintech product", "a platform for financial services")

## Practical Decisions

### Cadence

You mentioned every couple of months. That's a good rhythm — enough time for things to genuinely change between posts, and not so frequent that it becomes a burden. Bimonthly gives you six posts a year.

> **YOUR TURN:** Are you thinking strictly bimonthly, or more "when I have something worth saying"? Do you want to commit to a schedule publicly or keep it flexible?

### Platform

Where you publish shapes the audience and the tone. Your personal site (jakobhojgaard.com) gives you full control. LinkedIn reaches business/founder audiences. Substack or Medium builds a subscriber base. Twitter/X threads work for shorter takes that drive traffic to the full post.

> **YOUR TURN:** Are you publishing on jakobhojgaard.com, cross-posting elsewhere, or both? Do you already have an audience somewhere?

### Anonymity & Specificity

You're not naming the product, which is fine and arguably better for now. But think about how specific you'll be about yourself, your background, and the market you're in. More specificity builds more credibility, but also exposes more.

> **YOUR TURN:** How identifiable do you want to be? Are you comfortable saying "I'm building in financial services" or do you want to be even more general? Is there a reason beyond timing for not naming the product?
